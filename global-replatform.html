My Role:

Lead Designer

As Young Living's longest-tenured designer, I led all UX/UI efforts to unify the platforms into a single, scalable system. This project required balancing technical constraints with user needs while navigating a complex organizational structure.

End-to-End Design Owner:

Led product strategy, research, wireframes, design system, developer handoff, and design QA. Balancing user needs with internal team efficiency.

Cross-Functional Support:

Working closely with content managers, product managers, developers, and stakeholders to align on requirements, goals, and execution.

Accessibility Advocate:

Ensuring the new platform and CMS met ADA/WCAG compliance from the start, making accessibility a core principle rather than an afterthought.

The Problem:

Disparate Systems & High Costs

Young Living utilized three separate platforms across different markets. Each system has its own branding, functionality, and technical limitations, creating fragmented experiences for users and internal teams alike.

$5M

Annual cost of maintaining one main legacy system.

4-12 hours

Hours required for global content updates.

22

Global markets with inconsistent experiences.

Maintenance costs drained resources that could have funded innovation. Content bottlenecks slowed their ability to respond to market needs, while deteriorating infrastructure meant bug fixes consumed resources that should have gone into product improvements.

The Proposition:

North Star Prototype

To unify the platforms, I began designing our theoretically ideal experience. Working with a Product Manager, we defined our MVP and developed a phased rollout strategy for gradual market adoption.

Breaking the transformation into manageable phases decreases time to market, minimizes disruption, and aligns our teams on next best steps as we follow the north star.

A quick detour:

Custom CMS Subproject

A core component of this project will be a custom content management system. Integrated into the new platform, the new CMS will give content managers greater autonomy and efficiency.

Composable Components:

Enables content managers to build and update pages independently.

Built-in Accessibility:

Ensures all pages are ADA/WCAG-compliant by default, eliminating the need for manual fixes.

Massive Efficiency Gains:

Reduces content update time from 4–12 hours to just 15–30 minutes.

The Roadblocks:

And our approach to Problem-Solving

User Adoption Risk:

Introduced simplified, but familiar UI patterns with a gradual transition to ease the learning curve.

Resource Constraints:

Formed tiger teams with dedicated warrooms to balance daily operations with platform development.

Technical Limitations:

Built to temporarily integrate with legacy databases to ease the need to migrate all data at once.

The Unexpected Turn:

Cutting Sunk Cost & Shifting Focus

Midway through development, we realized that building a homegrown solution was stretching our resources and delaying timelines. After reassessing scalability and long-term costs, the executive team made the call to pivot toward customizing a third-party platform.

This shift meant rethinking our design priorities, working within vendor constraints, and finding new ways to deliver the same business and user value. By staying focused on our north star and remaining flexible, we chose a different approach to reach the same outcome.
